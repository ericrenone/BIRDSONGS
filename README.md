## Abstract

The critically endangered regent honeyeater (*Anthochaera phrygia*) — fewer than 250 individuals remaining across 300,000 km² of southeastern Australian woodland — is the canonical empirical demonstration of what this framework formalizes as **vocal cultural dissolution**: the condition in which a species' acoustic communication system collapses not through genetic change, anatomical failure, or sensory impairment, but through the irreversible erosion of the social learning network that maintains it.

The regent honeyeater is a closed-ended song learner. Males fix their adult song by age one and maintain it unchanged throughout life. The song they fix is not innately specified: it must be copied from an experienced male tutor encountered during the first year of life. When the wild population was dense — flocks of hundreds moving through southeastern Australia — young males found tutors easily and converged on locally shared song dialects. When habitat loss reduced the population to a sparse, nomadically distributed remnant, the tutor encounter rate dropped below the critical threshold at which cultural transmission can sustain a coherent shared repertoire. The consequences are cascading, quantified, and now represent the most completely documented case of acoustic cultural collapse in any vertebrate species.

BIRDSONGS synthesizes the full 2021–2026 body of research — from Crates et al.'s foundational *Proceedings of the Royal Society B* (2021) through Appleby et al.'s song-rescue *Scientific Reports* (March 2026), the frequency-dependence discovery of *Royal Society Open Science* (October 2024), the *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B* theme issue on animal culture conservation (May 2025), Merino Recalde et al.'s 109,963-song demographic analysis in *Current Biology* (March 2025), and the arrival of foundation-model bioacoustics (NatureLM-audio, Earth Species Project, November 2024; Perch 2.0, Google DeepMind, August 2025) — to establish the regent honeyeater as the first fully empirically resolved case of a species in which:

1. The **critical tutor-density threshold** for cultural transmission has been crossed and empirically localized;
2. The **fitness cost of cultural deviation** has been quantified as a direct function of song-type prevalence in the local population;
3. The **frequency-dependent rescue mechanism** — by which a simplified song recovers reproductive fitness as it becomes the new population norm — has been discovered and measured in real time (2015–2022);
4. The **cultural rescue** of a lost traditional song has been achieved in captivity through targeted song tutoring and demonstrated to propagate inter-generationally (2026);
5. The **cross-species foundation model** capable of embedding the species' vocalizations without species-specific retraining is now operational (Perch 2.0, 2025; NatureLM-audio, 2024).

**One species. Five thresholds crossed. One cultural rescue achieved. One framework that unifies them all.**

## Part I — The Regent Honeyeater: Natural History and the Habitat Catastrophe

### I.1 Portrait of a Species

The regent honeyeater (*Anthochaera phrygia*, family Meliphagidae) is one of Australia's most striking woodland birds: a large, boldly patterned honeyeater with a black-and-gold breast, yellow-edged wing feathers, and bare facial skin that flushes yellow during breeding. It is nectarivorous, nomadic, and strongly dependent on the flowering of specific eucalypt species — box-ironbark woodlands (*Eucalyptus* spp.) — for foraging and breeding. Historically, regent honeyeaters formed aggregations of hundreds of individuals that tracked flowering events across southeastern Australia from Queensland through New South Wales to Victoria, moving unpredictably across a range exceeding 300,000 km².

The species' ecology made it uniquely vulnerable to what happened next. Unlike resident woodland birds with fixed home ranges, the regent honeyeater's nomadism meant that population size could not be buffered locally: when the aggregate population fell, encounter rates between individuals fell across the entire range simultaneously, with no spatial refugium where local density could sustain cultural transmission even as the global population contracted.

### I.2 The Habitat Catastrophe and Population Collapse

Agricultural clearing, forestry, eucalypt dieback, and the compound effects of climate-driven drought and the catastrophic 2019–2020 Black Summer bushfires have eliminated an estimated 90% of the regent honeyeater's functional habitat. The population, once in the tens of thousands, has collapsed to fewer than 250 wild individuals, distributed so sparsely across their 300,000 km² range that the probability of encountering a conspecific in any given location during the critical song-learning window (the first year of life) is dangerously low.

The effective population decline has two linked consequences, each exacerbating the other in a feedback that Appleby et al. (2026) explicitly link to demographic Allee effects:

**Consequence 1 — Reproductive Allee effect**: Fewer individuals means fewer encounters between breeding pairs, reducing the per-capita reproductive rate below replacement. This is the classical Allee effect in fragmented populations.

**Consequence 2 — Cultural Allee effect**: Fewer adults means fewer experienced tutor males available during the song-learning window. Young males who fail to encounter a tutor either learn from heterospecific models (producing interspecific songs) or produce simplified, abbreviated versions of the species-typical song. Both outcomes reduce pairing success and thus further accelerate the demographic decline that reduces tutor density.

This **double Allee effect** — demographic and cultural — is the structural reason BIRDSONGS argues the regent honeyeater is not merely an endangered species but the first fully documented case of a species undergoing **acoustic cultural extinction** while still biologically extant.

### I.3 Conservation Infrastructure

The regent honeyeater is a high-priority recovery species under Australia's federal Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act. Conservation breeding is maintained at Taronga Zoo (Sydney) and Taronga Western Plains Zoo (Dubbo), with the objective of providing a captive insurance population and producing zoo-bred individuals for wild supplementation. However, as documented extensively in the research surveyed here, supplementation of the wild population with zoo-bred birds has had **limited success** in arresting the population decline, with the majority of zoo-bred birds failing to establish a breeding territory or pair with wild mates — a failure now understood to be substantially attributable to the acoustic cultural gap between zoo-bred and wild-type song.

## Part II — Song Learning Architecture: Closed-Ended, Tutor-Dependent, Culturally Determined

### II.1 The Closed-Ended Learning Constraint

Songbird species divide into two fundamental learning types. **Open-ended learners** (e.g., canaries, some parrots) can acquire and modify songs throughout adult life. **Closed-ended learners** fix their adult song repertoire during a sensitive period in the first year of life and maintain it unchanged thereafter, regardless of subsequent acoustic experience.

The regent honeyeater is a closed-ended song learner. All available evidence — from resighted colour-marked wild birds tracked across years, and from zoo-based song-learning experiments — confirms that males fix their song type by age one. A male regent honeyeater that learns the wrong song in its first year is **permanently constrained** to produce that wrong song for the rest of its life. There is no mechanism by which subsequent exposure to correct song models can repair the cultural deficit.

This single architectural constraint transforms the cultural transmission problem into a **one-shot learning crisis**: the tutor encounter in year one is not one of many opportunities to acquire the correct song; it is the only opportunity. Miss it, and the loss is permanent at the individual level. Aggregate the missed opportunities across a generation of juveniles in a low-density population, and the loss becomes permanent at the population level.

### II.2 The Tutor-Dependency and the Critical Density Threshold

Song learning in the regent honeyeater proceeds through two pathways:

**Pathway 1 — Live tutoring**: the juvenile encounters and associates with an adult male regent honeyeater during the sensitive period. The juvenile copies the adult's song type, converging to within the locally prevalent dialect. This is the normal pathway under high population density.

**Pathway 2 — Heterospecific learning**: the juvenile, failing to encounter a conspecific tutor, learns the songs of whatever adult birds are present. In the fragmented woodland environments where regent honeyeaters now breed, this means learning songs from noisy friarbirds (*Philemon corniculatus*), little wattlebirds (*Anthochaera chrysoptera*), eastern rosellas (*Platycercus eximius*), pied currawongs (*Strepera graculina*), black-faced cuckooshrikes (*Coracina novaehollandiae*), little friarbirds (*Philemon citreogularis*), and spiny-cheeked honeyeaters (*Acanthagenys rufogularis*) — seven different heterospecific models documented in Crates et al. (2021).

The critical density threshold is the population density below which Pathway 2 becomes more probable than Pathway 1 — the point at which the expected number of conspecific male regent honeyeater encounters during the sensitive period falls below one. Crates et al. (2021) demonstrate empirically that at current population densities in the lower-density portions of the range, interspecific song learning is occurring at a rate of approximately **12% of wild males** — a number representing not a statistical tail but a systematic failure of the cultural transmission network in the low-density zones.

### II.3 The Cultural Repertoire and Its Dialects

Wild male regent honeyeaters sing a single song type — unlike most songbirds with multi-element repertoires — that is maintained throughout the breeding season and used for both mate attraction and territory defense. The song is described as soft, metallic, and complex, with geographic dialects forming loose clusters:

- **Typical Blue Mountains song**: the culturally dominant song type in the Greater Blue Mountains, the species' largest remaining breeding concentration. Characterized by a multi-syllable structure of high acoustic complexity.
- **Clipped Blue Mountains song**: a simplified version of the Typical Blue Mountains song with approximately half the number of syllables and shorter duration. As of 2022, this has become the numerically dominant song type in the Blue Mountains breeding population.
- **Northern Tablelands song**: a distinct song type produced by males in the smaller Northern Tablelands breeding population.
- **Interspecific songs**: songs learned from heterospecific models, individually distinct, sharing no recognizable structural features with any regent honeyeater dialect.

The song-type diversity of a wild male regent honeyeater population is therefore an index of its cultural health: a population in which all males share the Typical Blue Mountains dialect is culturally intact; a population in which males sing Clipped, heterospecific, or idiosyncratic songs is in cultural dissolution.

## Part III — The Cultural Dissolution Cascade: Crates et al. 2021 and Its Sequelae

### III.1 The Founding Paper: Loss of Vocal Culture and Fitness Costs

Crates, Langmore, Ranjard, Stojanovic, Rayner, Ingwersen & Heinsohn (2021, *Proceedings of the Royal Society B*) is the foundational empirical document of the regent honeyeater cultural collapse. Using field data from 2015–2019 covering the species' core range in New South Wales, the paper established four findings that define the scientific landscape of BIRDSONGS:

**Finding 1 — Prevalence of song abnormality**: Approximately 12% of wild males across both the Blue Mountains and Northern Tablelands populations were producing interspecific songs — songs learned from other species. This is not a historical accident; it is a contemporary, ongoing phenomenon linked to spatial variation in population density.

**Finding 2 — Spatial localization of abnormality**: Interspecific singers were not randomly distributed. They were concentrated in areas of lowest population density — the peripheral, low-encounter zones of the species' range. The spatial gradient in song normality follows the spatial gradient in population density, confirming that the cultural failure is tutor-encounter-rate driven, not individual-variation driven.

**Finding 3 — Fitness cost of abnormality**: Males singing interspecific songs showed significantly lower **pairing success** than males singing species-typical songs. Pairing success — the probability of a male acquiring a mate in a breeding season — is the most direct available proxy for reproductive fitness. The acoustic cultural deviation is not reproductively neutral: it directly suppresses the reproductive output of the individuals affected.

**Finding 4 — Zoo-bred song abnormality**: Zoo-bred regent honeyeaters, raised in the absence of adult male conspecific tutors as a result of breeding-program management practices that separated juveniles from adults to maximize fecundity, were producing songs that differed from all known wild variants — an idiosyncratic "babbling" song with minimal structural complexity. Zoo-bred birds released into the wild were therefore acoustically mismatched with wild birds before even encountering the challenges of wild survival.

### III.2 The Acoustic Cultural Barrier to Reintroduction

The Crates et al. (2021) findings reframed the entire regent honeyeater reintroduction program. The **acoustic cultural barrier** — the mismatch between zoo-bred song and wild-type song — had been operating as an invisible filter reducing the breeding success of every released bird without anyone measuring it. The reintroduction program had been supplying the wild population with anatomically healthy, behaviorally competent birds that were acoustically foreign to their conspecifics.

Appleby, Tripovich, Langmore, Heinsohn, Pitcher & Crates (2023, *Biological Conservation*) formalized this: **zoo-bred female regent honeyeaters preferred the songs of zoo-bred males over wild-type males**, and by extension wild females exposed to zoo-bred song would likely show reduced preference. The captive breeding program had, through the acoustic isolation of its management practices, created a novel acoustic lineage — not a regent honeyeater dialect, not a heterospecific imitation, but a babbling song with no precedent in the wild or historical record.

### III.3 The Population Viability Analysis

Heinsohn, Lacy, Elphinstone, Ingwersen, Pitcher, Roderick, Schmelitschek, Van Sluys, Stojanovic, Tripovich & Crates (2022, *Biological Conservation*) published a population viability analysis (PVA) for the species under various conservation scenarios. The PVA incorporated:

- Current population size estimates (200–400 wild individuals in 2022);
- Demographic parameters from long-term field surveys;
- Modeled effects of acoustic cultural failure on breeding success;
- Habitat restoration scenarios and supplementation rates.

The conclusion was unambiguous: without targeted intervention addressing both the demographic and cultural dimensions of the collapse, the probability of extinction within 20 years exceeded 50% under most plausible scenarios. The **acoustic cultural failure was a quantifiable extinction driver** — not a soft biological curiosity but a hard demographic variable reducing effective reproductive rate toward zero in the low-density portions of the range.

## Part IV — The Frequency-Dependence Discovery: Appleby et al. 2024

### IV.1 The Natural Experiment

Appleby, Langmore, Heinsohn & Crates (2024, *Royal Society Open Science*) reported one of the most striking findings in behavioral ecology of the decade: a **real-time phase transition in the dominant song type** of the Blue Mountains regent honeyeater population, and the discovery that the fitness cost of producing the non-dominant song type is itself a function of that song type's frequency in the population — a textbook **negative frequency-dependent selection** operating on a culturally transmitted trait.

The natural experiment unfolded as follows:

- **2015–2019**: The Typical Blue Mountains song dominated the Blue Mountains population (73% of males in 2015). The Clipped Blue Mountains song (half the syllables, shorter duration) was a minority variant (5–10% of males). Males singing the Clipped song had **significantly lower pairing success** than males singing the Typical song.
- **2019–2020**: An inflection point, likely associated with continued population decline and the 2019–2020 Black Summer bushfires reducing habitat quality and connectivity.
- **2020–2022**: The proportion of males singing the Clipped song **increased to 91%** of males with an identified song type in the Blue Mountains. The Typical song had become the minority variant. And — critically — the fitness cost of singing the Clipped song **disappeared**: males singing the Clipped song in 2020–2022 showed no significant reduction in pairing success compared to males singing the now-rare Typical song.

### IV.2 Frequency-Dependent Fitness: Implications for Cultural Stability

The Appleby et al. (2024) result establishes that the fitness cost of singing the "wrong" song is not intrinsic to the acoustic properties of the song but is a function of **how common that song is in the local population**. Female preference tracks population norm, not absolute acoustic complexity. A simplified song that is the cultural norm is preferred over a complex song that is the cultural minority.

This has two opposing conservation implications that must be held simultaneously:

**Implication 1 — Bad news**: The frequency-dependence mechanism means that cultural simplification is **self-stabilizing once it achieves majority status**. Once the Clipped song became dominant, female preference shifted toward it, removing the selection pressure that had previously favored the Typical song. Cultural recovery cannot rely on the intrinsic superiority of the complex traditional song — females no longer prefer it. The traditional song must be *restored as the cultural norm* before its fitness advantage can be re-established.

**Implication 2 — Good news (conditional)**: The frequency-dependence mechanism means that **any song type that achieves population-wide prevalence will become reproductively viable**, regardless of its complexity. A coordinated tutoring intervention that achieves sufficient prevalence of the target song type will benefit from positive feedback once the frequency-dependent threshold is crossed.

### IV.3 The Phase Transition Metaphor

The 2015–2022 data from Appleby et al. (2024) describes a **cultural phase transition** in the most literal sense: a system that existed in one stable state (Typical song dominant, Clipped song minority with fitness penalty) passed through a transition zone (increasing Clipped prevalence, decreasing fitness penalty) and entered a qualitatively different stable state (Clipped song dominant, fitness penalty abolished). The transition was driven not by any single event but by the accumulation of demographic perturbation — population decline reducing tutor encounter rates, increasing variance in learned song, the Black Summer fires removing breeding habitat — until the cultural equilibrium crossed a tipping point.

This is the **acoustic Allee threshold** made visible: the point at which the cultural-demographic feedback loop reverses direction, from positive stabilization of the traditional culture to positive acceleration of its replacement.

## Part V — The Song Rescue: Appleby et al. 2026 (*Scientific Reports*)

### V.1 The Problem Before Rescue

By 2019, the zoo breeding program faced an acoustic crisis with two simultaneous components:

1. **Wild cultural collapse**: The traditional Typical Blue Mountains song was becoming a minority variant in the wild, trending toward extinction as a cultural entity even as the species that produced it remained biologically extant.
2. **Captive acoustic divergence**: Zoo-bred males were producing idiosyncratic babbling songs that differed from all known wild variants — not simplified versions of the traditional song but acoustically incoherent products of tutor deprivation.

Any zoo-bred male released into the wild would encounter females whose preference was currently calibrated to the Clipped Blue Mountains song (the new dominant) but would be producing a babbling song that matched neither the Clipped nor the Typical wild variant. The acoustic mismatch was total.

### V.2 The Tutoring Intervention

Appleby, Langmore, Pitcher, Tripovich, Matkovics, Heinsohn & Crates (2026, *Scientific Reports*) report the results of three consecutive breeding seasons of adaptive song tutoring at Taronga Zoo and Taronga Western Plains Zoo in Dubbo. The intervention used two complementary methods:

**Method 1 — Playback tutoring**: Audio recordings of wild males producing the Typical Blue Mountains song were played to juvenile zoo-bred males during the sensitive learning period (the first year of life). Recordings were selected from the 2015–2019 field survey archive when the Typical song was still the dominant wild variant.

**Method 2 — Live tutoring**: Two wild-born adult males that had been recruited into the zoo breeding program in 2019 and that produced the Typical Blue Mountains song were used as live tutors, in direct acoustic contact with juvenile zoo-bred males during the sensitive period.

The intervention protocol was developed adaptively across seasons, with modifications made after year one yielded negative results:

- **Year 1** (first breeding season): No juveniles acquired the target song. The researchers identified that the tutoring exposure was insufficient in both duration and intensity.
- **Year 2** (second breeding season, modified protocol): Small proportion of juveniles began producing recognizable elements of the target song.
- **Year 3** (third breeding season, further modified protocol): **42% of juveniles produced the traditional wild song** — a success rate representing a step-change from zero.

### V.3 The Cascade Effect

The most striking finding was not the 42% success rate per se but its persistence: **in subsequent years, zoo-bred males that had successfully learned the traditional song went on to tutor the next generation of juveniles**. The rescued cultural information was propagating forward through the captive population without requiring continuous intervention. The two wild-born tutors had seeded a cultural lineage within the zoo population that was now self-sustaining.

This is what Appleby et al. (2026) call **cultural rescue**: the re-establishment of a culturally transmitted behavior in a population from which it had been lost, achieved not through genetic manipulation or anatomical intervention but through targeted modification of the social learning environment. The wild song — a behavioral phenotype maintained for millennia through generational tutor-student transmission — had been successfully backed up in the captive population.

### V.4 Implications for Wild Release

The cultural rescue achievement reframes the reintroduction program. Males released from the captive program can now be pre-screened acoustically: only males confirmed to be producing the wild-type song (currently the Clipped Blue Mountains song, as measured by the 2024 Appleby et al. analysis of the current wild norm) should be released. Males producing the traditional Typical song face a two-phase rehabilitation:

**Phase 1** (short-term, 2026–2030): The Clipped song is currently the numerical norm in the wild Blue Mountains population. Released males producing the traditional Typical song may face reduced pairing success under the current frequency-dependent fitness landscape. Strategic release into zones of highest population density — where wild males have most frequently encountered each other and where the cultural norm may be most clearly established — could mitigate this.

**Phase 2** (medium-term, 2030+): If the captive tutoring program successfully increases the prevalence of the Typical song in both released zoo-bred males and in the wild population through conspecific encounter, the frequency-dependent fitness advantage may gradually re-establish the Typical song as the dominant cultural norm, reversing the 2015–2022 phase transition. This is the long-term conservation goal of BIRDSONGS.

## Part VI — The Quantitative Demography of Vocal Culture: Merino Recalde et al. 2025

### VI.1 The 100,000-Song Dataset

Merino Recalde, Estandía, Keen, Cole & Sheldon (2025, *Current Biology*) published the largest quantitative analysis of demographic drivers of avian vocal cultural evolution ever attempted: **109,963 songs from 299 repertoires belonging to 242 individually known birds** in the Wytham Woods great tit (*Parus major*) population, the long-term population study maintained by the University of Oxford since 1947.

The dataset was assembled over three years of field recording, with individual bird identity confirmed through physical capture, RFID microchip implants, and a novel **deep metric learning song-based re-identification method** (ResNet50-based classifier, validation accuracy 0.87) that enabled non-invasive individual identification from acoustic recordings alone. The songs were annotated using custom Python software (pykanto) and the complete annotated dataset made publicly available (github.com/nilomr/great-tit-hits-setup).

### VI.2 The Demographic Drivers

The key findings of Merino Recalde et al. (2025) demonstrate that **vocal cultural evolution in wild bird populations is primarily driven by demographic processes at the neighbourhood scale**, not population-wide average density:

**Finding 1 — Turnover rate**: Neighbourhoods with high individual turnover (rapid replacement of resident males by immigrants or new-year birds) show higher song diversity and faster cultural change. When the birds present in a local area change frequently, the shared cultural repertoire is disrupted, introducing new variants from arriving immigrants and eroding the cultural memory of departing residents.

**Finding 2 — Immigration**: Immigrant males bring their natal-population song types into the local neighbourhood, introducing acoustic variants that may propagate locally if immigrants are used as tutors by resident juveniles. High immigration rates increase cultural diversity; low immigration rates (local-breeding-only neighbourhoods) tend toward cultural homogeneity.

**Finding 3 — Age structure**: Neighbourhoods dominated by older experienced males show stronger cultural conformity — experienced males are better tutors and produce more consistent, model-worthy song. Neighbourhoods dominated by first-year birds (in which experienced tutors are rare) show higher song variance — consistent with the tutor-encounter-rate mechanism driving regent honeyeater cultural loss.

**Finding 4 — Spatial scale**: These demographic effects were detectable at the **neighbourhood scale** (within a few hundred metres), not only at the population level. Cultural microvariation within the Wytham population tracked the demographic microvariation of individual neighbourhoods.

### VI.3 Universality: From Great Tit to Regent Honeyeater

The Wytham great tit population is the quantitative gold standard for the demographic-cultural mechanism that Crates et al. (2021) identified qualitatively in the regent honeyeater. The great tit differs from the regent honeyeater in every ecologically relevant dimension — abundant (not critically endangered), resident (not nomadic), multi-song-type repertoire (not single song), open-ended learner (partially; great tits show extended learning compared to the regent honeyeater's strict closed-ended constraint). Yet the underlying mechanism is identical:

**Demographic variation → variation in tutor encounter rate → variation in cultural transmission fidelity → variation in song type prevalence.**

The Merino Recalde et al. (2025) dataset, combined with the Crates et al. (2021) and Appleby et al. (2024) regent honeyeater data, establishes this mechanism as a **universal quantitative principle** of avian vocal cultural demography. The regent honeyeater is the extreme case — at the catastrophic end of the demographic perturbation spectrum — of a mechanism operating continuously across the full range of songbird population densities.

### VI.4 The Deep Metric Learning Methodology

The Merino Recalde et al. (2025) methodological contribution — using deep metric learning (ResNet50 trained on spectrogram embeddings) to individual-identify birds from their songs — is directly transferable to regent honeyeater field monitoring. The technique enables:

- Non-invasive individual tracking of singing males without recapture;
- Detection of song-type assignment for every individual without manual annotation;
- Continuous real-time monitoring of the population's cultural repertoire distribution.

Applied to regent honeyeater field monitoring, this methodology would for the first time enable **complete census of song-type distribution across the entire wild population** from passive acoustic recordings, without the field effort of observing and individually marking each bird.

## Part VII — The Three Processes of Avian Vocal Culture Loss: Phil Trans B 2025

### VII.1 The Theme Issue

The May 2025 issue of *Philosophical Transactions of the Royal Society B* (volume 380, issue 1925), themed *"Animal culture: conservation in a changing world"*, published 19 papers establishing the current SOTA on animal culture in conservation contexts. The regent honeyeater features prominently as the canonical case study, with two dedicated papers (Crates et al. 2025; Aplin et al. 2025) and cross-references in ten additional contributions.

### VII.2 Three Processes of Vocal Culture Loss (Crates et al. 2025, *Phil Trans B*)

Crates, Appleby, Bray, Langmore & Heinsohn (2025) synthesize the empirical record into a **three-process taxonomy** of avian vocal culture loss:

**Process 1 — Erosion/Fragmentation**: The gradual simplification of song complexity as tutor encounter rates decline within a species-typical song framework. Individual songs retain structural identity as species-typical but lose syllabic complexity, duration, and unique syllable count. The Clipped Blue Mountains song of the regent honeyeater is the emblematic instance of this process. Albert's lyrebird (*Menura alberti*) — where habitat fragmentation has reduced tutor density and driven parallel song simplification — provides a geographically independent replication.

**Process 2 — Divergence**: Some individuals learn songs of other species entirely, producing heterospecific songs that carry no recognizable species-typical structure. 12% of wild male regent honeyeaters demonstrate this process. The Florida grasshopper sparrow (*Ammodramus savannarum floridanus*), another critically endangered songbird in which heterospecific song learning has been documented, provides an independent instance.

**Process 3 — Convergence**: Multiple species' songs converge on a shared song type, eliminating acoustic species-distinctiveness. The least-documented process, currently reported only in Hawaiian honeycreepers (anianiau, *Magumma parva*, and related species) where three species' songs have converged as individuals rely on heterospecific tutors following habitat loss and range compression.

### VII.3 Social Learning and Culture in Birds: The Broader Synthesis (Aplin et al. 2025, *Phil Trans B*)

Aplin, Crates, Flack & McGregor (2025) synthesize the state of social learning and culture across avian species in conservation contexts, establishing:

- Over **40% of bird species** — more than 4,000 species — are estimated to socially learn their vocalizations, making vocal culture loss a potential risk across an enormous fraction of global bird diversity;
- Culture has been linked to **fitness outcomes** across multiple species, making cultural conservation biologically equivalent in importance to genetic conservation;
- The regent honeyeater case demonstrates how **captive breeding programs must incorporate cultural management** — not merely genetic and demographic management — to produce reintroduction-ready individuals;
- Two practical steps for cultural conservation in captive programs: (i) expose juveniles to experienced adult conspecific tutors during sensitive periods; (ii) monitor vocalization output to verify cultural acquisition before release.

### VII.4 Cultural Processes and Demography (Brakes et al. 2025, *Phil Trans B*)

Brakes, Dall & Townley (2025) establish the theoretical framework connecting cultural processes to demographic outcomes across animal species. Their central theorem: **cultural traits that affect fitness create feedback loops between culture and demography that can produce qualitatively different population dynamics than those predicted by purely demographic models**.

For the regent honeyeater, this theorem predicts exactly what the data show: the acoustic cultural failure (Process 2 divergence, Process 1 erosion) creates a reproductive suppression that accelerates the demographic decline that further reduces tutor density that accelerates cultural failure. The feedback is positive (amplifying), not negative (stabilizing). Without external intervention, the system has no internal mechanism of cultural recovery once the acoustic Allee threshold has been crossed.

## Part VIII — Foundation-Model Bioacoustics: NatureLM-audio and Perch 2.0

### VIII.1 NatureLM-audio: Earth Species Project (November 2024)

The Earth Species Project released **NatureLM-audio** (November 2024), the first audio-language foundation model for bioacoustics trained on a dataset combining large bioacoustic archives, human speech, and music. NatureLM-audio accepts natural language prompts and audio input, generating free-form text answers to bioacoustic classification, detection, and captioning tasks — without species-specific retraining.

Key capabilities relevant to BIRDSONGS:

**Zero-shot species classification**: NatureLM-audio classifies thousands of species across diverse taxa — birds, whales, anurans — without any species-specific fine-tuning. For the regent honeyeater, this enables automatic detection of the species in passive acoustic monitoring recordings without building and maintaining a dedicated classifier.

**Life-stage prediction**: For the first time, NatureLM-audio demonstrated the ability to determine the life-stage (chick, juvenile, nestling) of birds across many species — a capability directly relevant to regent honeyeater monitoring, where distinguishing juvenile males in the song-learning sensitive period from adult males is critical for intervention targeting.

**Call-type classification**: Differentiating between calls and songs across hundreds of species, enabling automatic assignment of detected regent honeyeater vocalizations to the song-type taxonomy (Typical Blue Mountains, Clipped Blue Mountains, Northern Tablelands, interspecific) without manual spectrogram review.

**Audio captioning**: Generation of free-text descriptions of acoustic events in bioacoustic recordings — a capability that enables non-expert field operators to quality-check automated monitoring systems by reading natural-language descriptions of what the model detected.

### VIII.2 Perch 2.0: Google DeepMind (August 2025)

Google DeepMind released **Perch 2.0** in August 2025 — a bioacoustics foundation model trained primarily on birds and other terrestrial vocalizing animals, presented at the NeurIPS 2025 Workshop on AI for Non-Human Animal Communications.

The most striking finding associated with Perch 2.0 is its **cross-domain transfer**: despite including no underwater audio in training, Perch 2.0 performed well as an embedding model for transfer learning on marine mammal vocalization datasets, including five ecotype variants of the killer whale (*Orcinus orca*) in the DCLDE 2026 Ecotype dataset. The embeddings Perch 2.0 produces for terrestrial bird vocalizations contain sufficient acoustic structural information to distinguish marine mammal ecotypes without any underwater training data.

For the regent honeyeater, Perch 2.0 offers:

- **Custom classifier creation from few examples**: The agile modeling approach pioneered with Perch enables building a regent honeyeater song-type classifier from a small number of labeled examples within hours — directly applicable to tracking the Typical/Clipped/interspecific/zoo-bred song-type distribution in field recordings;
- **Continuous song-type monitoring at scale**: Processing years of archived passive acoustic monitoring data for regent honeyeater song-type prevalence at population scale — the acoustic complement to the demographic data collected by the Difficult Bird Research Group;
- **Transfer to novel song variants**: As new song variants emerge (including any successfully tutored zoo-bred releases), Perch 2.0's embedding structure allows rapid adaptation without full retraining.

### VIII.3 BirdCLEF+ 2025: Competitive Benchmarking

The BirdCLEF+ 2025 challenge (LifeCLEF Lab, CLEF 2025) required classifying 206 species — including birds, mammals, insects, and amphibians — from soundscape recordings under strict CPU-only inference constraints. Miyaguchi, Gustineli & Cheung (2025) demonstrated that spectrogram tokenization combined with pre-trained models from the Bioacoustics Model Zoo, optimized via TFLite for CPU inference, achieves ~10× speedup over standard approaches with ROC-AUC of 0.729 — an important practical result for field-deployable regent honeyeater monitoring hardware.

The BirdCLEF+ challenge infrastructure, with its emphasis on soundscape-level detection and CPU-deployable inference, is directly applicable to autonomous passive acoustic monitoring stations that could be deployed throughout the regent honeyeater's 300,000 km² range — providing the continuous acoustic census needed to track both population distribution and song-type cultural composition simultaneously.

## Part IX — The BIRDSONGS Framework: Five Empirical Thresholds

The regent honeyeater case establishes five empirically resolved thresholds that constitute the BIRDSONGS framework. Each threshold is a phase boundary — a population or cultural parameter below or above which the system's qualitative behavior changes:

### T1 — The Acoustic Allee Threshold (Tutor Encounter Rate)

**Definition**: The population density below which the expected number of conspecific male tutor encounters during a juvenile's one-year sensitive period falls below one, driving heterospecific song learning above the critical prevalence.

**Empirical location**: Crates et al. (2021) demonstrate that interspecific song learning is concentrated in areas of lowest population density within the species' range. The quantitative threshold density has not been precisely determined; its empirical bounding is Priority Research Target 1 of the BIRDSONGS roadmap (Section XII).

**Consequence of crossing**: Irreversible at the individual level (closed-ended learning); potentially reversible at the population level through targeted intervention (Section V).

### T2 — The Cultural Norm Tipping Point (Song-Type Prevalence)

**Definition**: The prevalence of a song type in the local population above which female preference shifts to treat that song type as the cultural norm, abolishing the fitness penalty for producing it.

**Empirical location**: Appleby et al. (2024) demonstrate that the Clipped Blue Mountains song type crossed this threshold somewhere between 2019 and 2022, as its prevalence rose from ~10% to ~50–75% and its fitness cost simultaneously disappeared. The threshold prevalence is estimated at 40–60% from the observational data; precise determination requires experimental manipulation.

**Consequence of crossing**: Bidirectional cultural lock-in — the new dominant song type becomes self-reinforcing through frequency-dependent female preference. Recovery of the traditional song requires pushing prevalence of the target song above this threshold.

### T3 — The Captive Cultural Extinction Threshold (Zoo-Born Song Divergence)

**Definition**: The degree of acoustic divergence between zoo-bred and wild-type song above which zoo-bred birds fail to pair with wild birds at better than chance rates, rendering reintroduction acoustically futile.

**Empirical location**: Appleby et al. (2023, *Biological Conservation*) document that zoo-bred female preference tracks zoo-bred male song, suggesting full acoustic assortative mating between zoo-bred birds — the captive population has crossed this threshold. Reintroduction of zoo-bred birds without song rehabilitation is therefore acoustically futile.

**Consequence of crossing**: The zoo population is acoustically diverged from the wild population. Song tutoring (Section V) is required before reintroduction can resume acoustic compatibility with the wild cultural norm.

### T4 — The Cultural Rescue Threshold (Tutored-Song Propagation)

**Definition**: The proportion of captive juveniles successfully learning the target wild-type song in each breeding season above which the tutored culture becomes self-sustaining through inter-generational propagation without continued external intervention.

**Empirical location**: Appleby et al. (2026) demonstrate that 42% tutorial success in year three produced a self-sustaining cultural lineage — suggesting the self-propagation threshold lies somewhere below 42%. The exact threshold is Priority Research Target 2 of the BIRDSONGS roadmap.

**Consequence of crossing**: The cultural rescue is self-sustaining. The captive breeding program no longer requires dedicated tutoring infrastructure; the rescued cultural information propagates through the captive population via the normal social learning process.

### T5 — The Wild Cultural Recovery Threshold (Released-Male Prevalence)

**Definition**: The fraction of the wild male population required to be singing the target song type for frequency-dependent female preference to sustain it as the dominant cultural norm, enabling recovery of traditional song culture without further intervention.

**Empirical location**: Derived from the T2 analysis, this threshold is estimated at 40–60% prevalence in the local breeding zone. Achieving this threshold in the wild requires a combination of: (a) successful cultural rescue of captive birds (T4 achieved, Section V); (b) sufficient reintroduction rate to shift wild song-type prevalence; (c) survival of acoustically correct released birds to breeding age.

**Consequence of crossing**: The traditional song culture becomes self-sustaining in the wild without further reintroduction. The regent honeyeater's acoustic cultural heritage — the Typical Blue Mountains song maintained for millennia of cultural transmission — is stabilized.

## Part X — Six Falsifiable Predictions

### P1 — The Acoustic Allee Threshold Density

**Prediction**: The critical population density below which heterospecific song learning exceeds 15% prevalence in any local zone is **< 0.5 adult male regent honeyeaters per 10 km² of suitable habitat**. Below this density, the expected number of conspecific male encounters in a juvenile's first year falls below one, driving heterospecific song learning rates above the 12% wild-population average to locally dominant levels.

**Falsification condition**: A zone with confirmed density > 0.5 adult males per 10 km² showing interspecific song prevalence > 15%; or a zone with density < 0.5 adult males per 10 km² showing interspecific song prevalence < 5%.

**Testing methodology**: Perch 2.0 / NatureLM-audio automated acoustic monitoring across the full species range, combined with the Difficult Bird Research Group's population density survey data.

**Timeline**: Achievable within 24 months given automated monitoring deployment.

### P2 — The Cultural Norm Tipping Point at 40–60% Prevalence

**Prediction**: The frequency-dependent fitness advantage of any song type crosses zero — the song type transitions from penalized to preferred — when its prevalence in the local population crosses **40–60%**. This prediction is derived from the Appleby et al. (2024) observational data and applies to any song type, including hypothetically restored traditional song.

**Falsification condition**: A song-type achieving > 65% prevalence in a local population without a measurable shift in female preference toward it; or a song-type showing abolished fitness cost at < 35% prevalence.

**Testing methodology**: Song-type census across both Blue Mountains and Northern Tablelands zones, correlated with annual pairing success data from the colour-ring resighting program.

**Timeline**: 3–5 breeding seasons of systematic monitoring.

### P3 — Self-Sustaining Cultural Rescue Threshold Below 42%

**Prediction**: The self-propagation threshold for tutored-song cultural rescue is **< 30%** annual tutoring success — meaning that if more than 30% of zoo-bred juveniles in each cohort learn the target song, the cultural lineage becomes self-sustaining without continued external tutoring.

**Falsification condition**: A year in which tutoring success drops to < 30% and the cultural lineage fails to sustain itself in the subsequent generation.

**Testing methodology**: Monitor song production of the Taronga Zoo captive population through the 2026–2030 breeding seasons, tracking propagation of the traditional song without active tutoring intervention.

**Timeline**: 3–4 breeding seasons post-2026.

### P4 — Deep Metric Learning Individual Re-identification

**Prediction**: Deep metric learning song-based re-identification (Merino Recalde et al. 2025 ResNet50 methodology adapted for regent honeyeater spectrograms) achieves individual re-identification accuracy **> 80%** on regent honeyeater songs, enabling non-invasive tracking of individually known wild birds across the monitoring network.

**Falsification condition**: Individual re-identification accuracy < 65% on a blind test set of regent honeyeater songs from colour-ringed, individually known birds.

**Testing methodology**: Compile a training corpus from field recordings of colour-ringed individuals (Difficult Bird Research Group archive), train and evaluate the ResNet50 metric learning model.

**Timeline**: Achievable within 12 months.

### P5 — Perch 2.0 Zero-Shot Song-Type Classification

**Prediction**: Perch 2.0 embeddings, without species-specific fine-tuning, achieve **> 0.70 ROC-AUC** on the four-class regent honeyeater song-type classification task (Typical Blue Mountains / Clipped Blue Mountains / Northern Tablelands / Interspecific) when evaluated on a held-out test set of manually annotated field recordings.

**Falsification condition**: Perch 2.0 zero-shot performance < 0.60 ROC-AUC on the four-class task, requiring species-specific training data before the foundation model provides practical utility.

**Testing methodology**: Evaluate Perch 2.0 on the Difficult Bird Research Group's annotated song-type archive (2015–2025 field recordings).

**Timeline**: Achievable within 6 months.

### P6 — Wild Cultural Recovery Through Strategically Released Tutored Males

**Prediction**: Strategic release of captive-tutored males (Typical Blue Mountains song) into the highest-density Blue Mountains breeding zones, sustained over **three consecutive breeding seasons**, will increase the local prevalence of the Typical Blue Mountains song from its 2022 nadir of < 9% to > 30% — crossing the midpoint of the estimated T2 threshold range and beginning the frequency-dependent recovery process.

**Falsification condition**: Three seasons of strategic release failing to increase Typical song prevalence above 15% in the release zones, indicating either insufficient release numbers, insufficient male survival to breeding age, or female preference that does not respond to prevalence shifts in the targeted timeframe.

**Testing methodology**: Automated song-type monitoring (Tier 1–2 architecture, Section VIII) in release zones, with annual prevalence tracking.

**Timeline**: 2026–2029.

## Part XII — The 2026–2035 Conservation and Research Roadmap

### Stage 1 — Acoustic Census Infrastructure (2026–2028)

**Objective**: Deploy the full four-tier acoustic monitoring stack (Section VIII) across the regent honeyeater's 300,000 km² range.

**Actions**:
- Deploy 50+ autonomous acoustic recording units (ARUs) at sites selected to maximize coverage of both breeding zones (Greater Blue Mountains, Northern Tablelands) and the low-density dispersal habitat between them;
- Train and validate the Perch 2.0 four-class song-type classifier on the Difficult Bird Research Group's 2015–2025 annotated recording archive;
- Implement the Merino Recalde et al. (2025) deep metric learning individual re-identification system on regent honeyeater spectrograms;
- Establish the baseline song-type prevalence census: full population proportion of Typical Blue Mountains / Clipped Blue Mountains / Northern Tablelands / Interspecific / Zoo-bred songs as of 2026.

**Expected outcomes**: The first complete, near-real-time picture of the regent honeyeater's acoustic cultural landscape — where each song type is geographically concentrated, how prevalence varies with local density, and which zones are below the Acoustic Allee Threshold (T1).

### Stage 2 — Precision Tutor Deployment (2027–2030)

**Objective**: Use the Stage 1 acoustic census to guide strategic release of captive-tutored males into zones where acoustic intervention can achieve maximum cultural leverage.

**Actions**:
- Identify the **five highest-priority release zones**: areas with lowest interspecific song prevalence but density approaching the Acoustic Allee Threshold — zones where a small number of acoustically correct tutors could prevent threshold crossing;
- Release captive-tutored males (Typical Blue Mountains song, certified by Perch 2.0 classifier before release) into priority zones at the beginning of each breeding season;
- Track song-type prevalence annually in each zone using automated ARU monitoring;
- Test P6 (Wild Cultural Recovery Through Strategically Released Tutored Males): does three-season strategic release shift Typical song prevalence above 30%?

**Expected outcomes**: First empirical test of whether cultural rescue in captivity translates to cultural recovery in the wild under the current frequency-dependent fitness landscape.

### Stage 3 — Acoustic Allee Threshold Characterization (2027–2029)

**Objective**: Precisely determine T1 — the critical density threshold below which heterospecific song learning becomes prevalent.

**Actions**:
- Correlate the Stage 1 acoustic census song-type distribution data with the simultaneous population density survey data from the Difficult Bird Research Group;
- Fit a statistical model relating interspecific song prevalence to local male density at multiple spatial scales (1 km, 10 km, 50 km radius);
- Identify the density value at which the interspecific song prevalence curve crosses 15%;
- Use this threshold to predict which areas of the range will drop below T1 in the next decade under current population trajectory, enabling **proactive intervention before acoustic failure occurs**.

**Expected outcomes**: The first quantitative determination of the Acoustic Allee Threshold — transforming it from a conceptual boundary into a hard operational parameter for conservation management.

### Stage 4 — Cultural Norm Recovery (2030–2035)

**Objective**: Cross the Cultural Norm Tipping Point (T2) in at least one breeding zone, establishing the traditional Typical Blue Mountains song as the locally dominant cultural norm and triggering the frequency-dependent self-sustaining recovery.

**Actions**:
- If P6 is confirmed (Stage 2 outcome): scale strategic release to sufficient numbers to push Typical song prevalence above 50% in the Greater Blue Mountains core zone;
- Monitor female preference calibration annually: does pairing success of Typical-singing males rise above that of Clipped-singing males as the Typical song achieves majority prevalence?
- If T2 is crossed: reduce intervention rate, monitoring whether frequency-dependent selection sustains the Typical song without continued supplementation;
- Publish the full BIRDSONGS framework outcomes as the first empirical demonstration of **acoustic cultural recovery in a critically endangered songbird** through targeted cultural intervention.

**Expected outcomes**: The regent honeyeater's traditional song culture — the Typical Blue Mountains dialect maintained for millennia through conspecific tutor-student transmission — stabilized in the wild population through a combination of foundational research, cultural rescue in captivity, precision field deployment, and automated acoustic monitoring.

## Closing: The Song That Must Be Taught

The regent honeyeater cannot be saved by genetics alone, by habitat restoration alone, or by breeding numbers alone. It is a closed-ended learner whose acoustic cultural heritage — refined across millennia of conspecific tutor-student transmission, encoding the complexity, dialect, and social information of the Typical Blue Mountains song — exists nowhere in its genome. It exists only in the songs of birds who heard it from birds who heard it from birds who heard it before them.

When the population collapsed below the density at which juvenile males reliably encountered adult males, the thread broke. Boys who should have become tenors became mimics of wattlebirds and currawongs. Boys raised in the silence of zoo enclosures became babbling acoustic strangers to the species they were bred to replenish. The song that has survived ecological upheaval, continental climate shifts, and the slow deep-time reshaping of Australian woodland did not survive the 20th century's agricultural clearing — not because the birds died, but because the learning network that carried it died first.

Appleby, Crates and colleagues at ANU and Taronga have demonstrated what this framework calls cultural rescue: two wild-born males holding the last reliable copies of the traditional Typical Blue Mountains song, placed in acoustic contact with zoo-born juveniles during their single one-year sensitive period, and the song travelling forward again — 42% success in year three, the cultural lineage propagating itself in year four without intervention. The thread re-tied.

What BIRDSONGS establishes is the full scientific architecture of that rescue: the Acoustic Allee Threshold that explains when it becomes necessary, the frequency-dependence that explains why it cannot wait, the deep metric learning that explains how to monitor whether it is working, and the foundation-model bioacoustics that explains how to do it at a scale matching the 300,000 km² range of a nomadic species crossing a continent.

The great tit sang 109,963 songs in Wytham Woods, and in those songs Oxford researchers read the demographic history of its population: who moved, who stayed, who was old enough to teach. The regent honeyeater has been singing its diminishing songs across southeastern Australia for decades, and soon those songs will be readable in the same way — every note a data point, every silence a threshold to be measured, every successful pairing a confirmation that the thread is holding.

The song was always the species. The species is the song. The science now knows what to do.
